What September Data Reveals About Boston's Green Line

Rush hour on the Green Line is a Bostonian’s definition of frustration. At Prudential Station, the platform crowds with commuters, students, and shoppers as another two-car train pulls in. To no one's surprise, it’s already packed to capacity. A few determined riders squeeze on, but the rest wait, hoping the next train will offer some relief.

““The Green Line is by far the most complicated line,” says Seth Kaplan, a labs co-lead from TransitMatters, an organization dedicated to analyzing transit data and improving public transit in Boston. Kaplan points to the challenges created by the Green Line’s intricate design, where multiple branches converge into a single file line with just one track in each direction. This design leaves little room for flexibility during disruptions. “If a train is stuck at Copley, every train behind it isn’t going anywhere.”.

In September 2024, over 1.5 million passengers entered gated Green Line stations, a surge fueled by returning students and a revitalized post-pandemic city. However, these figures tell only part of the story. Above-ground stops on the B, C, D, and E Lines rely on onboard fare collection, meaning untapped entries aren’t counted, potentially adding thousands more riders to the system who are evading the fares.

The average wait time for a Green Line train barely changed during rush hour, even though tapped entries saw a spike of ridership. The first stops of each line may show missing data because the dataset is based on times between trains, and data for the initial segment may not be available.

It is also important to note that stations like Copley, Arlington, and North Station have multiple lines (B, C, D, E) that travel through the station, so these times do not accurately reflect the time you would be waiting depending on where you are going. But according to the MBTA, the green line ranks lowest in percentage of accurate predictions, with all 4 scoring below 73% accurate.

Percentage Accuracy of Prediction Across Different MBTA Lines

The MBTA says: "We consider a prediction to be accurate when a train or bus arrives within minutes of a prediction. The exact number of minutes depends on how far out into the future a prediction is." Even as the data reveals critical flaws, it also highlights the limitations of the MBTA’s current metrics. Above-ground stops on the B, C, and E Lines are unmeasured due to outdated onboard payment systems. Additionally, gated entries represent only passengers entering the Green Line system—they do not account for direction. Approximately half of these riders are traveling eastbound (inbound) and the other half westbound (outbound).

Multiple questions arise. Why are trains not coming on time? Shouldn't more trains service the increase of riders during rush hour? “The Green Line runs on headway management rather than a strict schedule,” Kaplan explains. "Unlike other lines that dispatch trains at specific times, the Green Line releases trains at intervals, adjusting for delays. It avoids bunching, but this also means more variance in times."

Delays as a result of disabeled trains are a large part of the problem the Green Line is currently facing. The MBTA currently tweets when a line is delayed or down, and through looking at tweets from the month of September, 43% of all tweets indicating a Green Line delay of at least 10 minutes, were because of a disabled train. Out-dated trains causing significant delays are rubbing salt into the wound that is the congestion problem on Boston's transit system. The MBTA cannot continue to put bandaids on the gapping wounds that exist on the T.

The consequences of the Green Line’s shortcomings extend far beyond inconvenience. Transit inefficiencies disrupt productivity, push riders into cars, and exacerbate Boston’s already infamous traffic congestion. “The T needs to convince riders to choose transit,” says Kaplan. “It needs to be the most efficient way to get around, especially for those who have other options.”

The MBTA promises relief with new Type 10 Green Line trains set to debut by 2027. These larger, more accessible cars will increase capacity by nearly 30%, a much-needed improvement. However, systemic issues like signal problems and power failures remain significant barriers, and if the new trains are not the answer to these problems, the Green Line will only improve minimally. “Fixing all the signal and power issues would transform the Green Line overnight,” says Kaplan.

Boston’s transit system stands at a crossroads. The Green Line’s historic charm should not come at the cost of modern reliability. With predictable surges in ridership and mounting systemic issues, the MBTA must act decisively to modernize infrastructure, increase train frequencies during rush hours, and implement more accurate ridership metrics.

After a long day at work, T riders should not have to endure the stress of not only wondering when their train is going to come, but then having to squeeze on an insanely over packed train that only fits 300 people, but is probably carrying closer to 350. At a stop such as Prudential, a train coming, at an average, every 7-8 minutes during rush hour, is unnaceptable, especially when these trains are rolling in mostly full already.

Public engagement is key. “Public comment is everything. Agencies like the MBTA need to hear from riders to understand where they’re falling short and how to improve.” Rider feedback has already driven changes, such as eliminating slow zones and redesigning bus routes. Sustained advocacy will be critical to ensuring Boston’s transit system evolves to meet the city’s needs.

The Green Line is a vital lifeline for Boston, connecting neighborhoods, universities, and businesses. But if the MBTA cannot address its current failings, it risks leaving the city’s future stuck on the platform, waiting for a train that never arrives.
